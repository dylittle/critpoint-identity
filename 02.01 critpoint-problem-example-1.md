# The Lightbulb Problem
## A Worked Example: When a System Rebuild Makes Things Worse

*Names and identifying details have been removed. The pattern is real.*

---

### What the Team Thought the Problem Was

Operations was struggling to manage which clients should be receiving services at any given time. The existing system was imprecise and hard to enforce. The proposed fix was straightforward: redefine "active status" to mean "in good standing according to operations," document the criteria that determined good standing, and build automation to switch the active flag on and off based on whether those criteria were met.

The logic was clean. The requirements were documented. The engineers built what they were asked to build.

---

### What the Actual Problem Was

Nobody had distinguished between two fundamentally different concepts that the word "active" was being asked to carry simultaneously:

- **Whether services were currently being rendered** to a client
- **Whether services should be rendered** to a client

Those are not the same thing. A client can be in good standing — contractually current, no outstanding issues — and still have services temporarily suspended. A client can have services running while a good-standing review is underway. Collapsing both meanings into a single flag meant the system could never represent either one accurately.

The gap went deeper. There were actually several distinct services that could be turned on and off independently — but the model treated them as one. And because the contract itself was tied to this imprecise active flag, the system had no way to represent a contract that existed but whose services were currently suspended. When the flag went off, the contract effectively disappeared from the backend.

A deeper consequence emerged from the collapsed model: there was no clean authority boundary between Operations and the backend system. Operations held the authoritative business judgment — they knew which clients should be receiving services and why. The backend held the system state — it determined what services were actually being delivered. Because contract and status had never been modeled separately, these two authorities were in permanent conflict. Engineering resolved this the only way available to them: by declaring the backend the source of truth and defining "active" as whatever the system said it was. Operations owned the business decision. Engineering owned the definition. Neither owned the concept — because the concept had never been properly defined.

The team was also modeling the operational process as a linear funnel — a cascade of different teams each responsible for a sequential step. In reality, the checks were independent. Different teams were responsible for different validations that had no dependency on each other. Modeling them as a series created coordination overhead and bottlenecks that didn't need to exist.

Finally, there was an observability problem nobody wanted to name: operations teams frequently couldn't say with certainty whether a given criterion had actually been met. The new system would require them to make calls they didn't have the information to make.

---

### What Was Called Out, and When

Before the build was complete, all of these issues were identified and raised explicitly.

The contract-versus-status problem was described this way: the proposed solution was like unscrewing a lightbulb every time services were suspended, and swapping lightbulbs when contracts changed. The recommendation was different — turn off the light switch when services are suspended, but keep the lightbulb in the socket. That way the system always knows what contract is in place, regardless of whether services are currently running.

The funnel-versus-checkbox problem was named directly: operational workflows should be modeled as parallel independent checks, not a sequential cascade. Each team owns its own box. When their box is checked, they're done — regardless of what other teams are doing.

The simplifications required for an initial release were acknowledged as necessary — but the recommendation was to make them explicit and documented, because the gaps they created would surface quickly once operations teams started making real decisions under uncertainty.

---

### What Happened

The recommendations were not adopted. The organizational conditions — timeline pressure, existing technical debt, and a model that had been in place for a decade — made it easier to build within the existing conceptual structure than to stop and redesign it. The engineers who built this were skilled. The operations managers who defined the requirements understood their domain deeply. The failure wasn't capability — it was that nobody's role included resolving the conceptual layer before the build started. That work fell between the cracks, as it usually does.

In the short term, things felt better. Teams had clearer documentation of what they were supposed to do. There was a sense of progress and shared understanding. The system looked more organized than what it replaced.

Over time, the underlying value continued to deteriorate for the same structural reasons. The system couldn't distinguish a suspended contract from a terminated one. Good-standing issues couldn't be diagnosed by cause because all causes fed into the same flag. Operational teams still couldn't get clear answers about specific client status without coordinating across multiple groups. The rebuild had produced a cleaner version of the same broken foundation.

---

### What Would Have Been Different

A model that separated contract from service status would have allowed services to be suspended without the client disappearing from revenue tracking. Leadership could have made accurate decisions about which clients were worth the operational investment to recover.

Modeling good-standing checks as independent parallel validations would have allowed each operations team to work without coordinating with the others. Bottlenecks could have been identified and addressed without cascading delays.

Explicit LTV estimates by good-standing issue type would have allowed the business to prioritize recovery efforts where the return justified the cost.

And the observability gap — acknowledged but left implicit — would have been treated as a first-class problem to solve rather than a known limitation to work around.

None of this required a fundamentally different technology stack. It required a fundamentally different model of the domain before anything was built.

---

### The Pattern This Illustrates

The team built exactly what they were asked to build. The requirements were clear. The engineering was competent. The project was delivered on time.

The system still failed — not because of execution, but because the concepts underneath the requirements were never made precise. "Active" meant two different things. "Good standing" was treated as a single dimension when it was actually several independent ones. "Contract" was collapsed into "status" when they needed to be separate.

When the concepts are wrong, the requirements are wrong. When the requirements are wrong, the build is wrong — no matter how well it's executed.

The work that prevents this isn't a better requirements process. It's modeling the domain precisely enough that the concepts are right before anyone writes a single requirement.
